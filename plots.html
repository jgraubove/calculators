About the App

Understanding Climate Metrics for Heritage Spaces

There are 5 visualisations all preventive conservators should know how to read:

  • Plot 1: Time Series - See how temperature and relative humidity change sequentially over time.
  • Plot 2: Scatterplot - See how T and RH are related.
  • Plot 3: Psychrometric Space Map - See absolute moisture levels, useful to infer the potential for heating and dehumidification.
  • Plot 4: Frequency Histograms - See the statistical counts and spread of measurement occurrences.
  • Plot 5: Period Barplot - See trends by grouping microclimatic variables into years or months.
  • Plot 6: Density Heatmap - Ok, this one is uncommon. But it is powerful and interpreting heatmaps is a useful skill. Allows to see the importance of clusters of overlapping datapoints.

To make these interpretations clear and effective, the application utilizes the ALEC framework. This is a simple way to remember how to structure the description of a plot:

1. Axes - First state the meaning of each axis.
2. Lines - Describe what the lines and points mean.
3. Example - Illustrate the meaning of the lines with an example datapoint.
4. Conclusion - Always end with an overall take-home message.

Plot 1: Environmental Time Series profile: This is a fundamental map in Preventive Conservation. It shows the evolution of temperature (red curve, left vertical axis) and relative humidity (blue curve, right vertical axis) sequentially over time (horizontal axis). Dashed horizontal bounds have been overlayed showing your active compliance thresholds.

As a general rule, plots should be read and explained as follows. First, identify the axes. Here the horizontal axis shows time, and the vertical ones show $T$ and $RH$. Second, describe the lines or points. Here the lines describe the change in $T$ (red) and $RH$ (blue). Third, it is often useful to include an example, in this case it can be pointed out that frequently $RH$ decreases when $T$ increases. Finally, end with a take home message, for example that the environment is changeable or it shows remarkable seasonality.

I like to call this method ALEC, for Axes, Lines, Example, Conclusion.

Plot 2: Bivariate Scatter distribution: This visualization translates raw concurrent datasets into a spatial pattern. A transparent target bounding box is overlaid to show how many logging points drifted outside the recommended zone.

To read this plot with ALEC: First, check the Axes. The horizontal axis represents Dry Bulb Temperature ($T$ in $^\circ\text{C}$), and the vertical axis represents Relative Humidity ($RH$ in $\%$). Second, describe the Lines/Points. Instead of sequential curves, each individual dot represents a single, concurrent $T$ and $RH$ logging measurement. Third, locate a clear Example. For instance, a highly dense, circular cluster of dots around $20\text{ }^\circ\text{C}$ and $50\%$ indicates a stable, well-controlled target microclimate, while dots wandering out into the margins (e.g., bottom-right or top-left corners) represent notable climatic stress events. Finally, draw a Conclusion. The overall shape and dispersion of this "point cloud" immediately reveals if the building's thermal envelope keeps the collection safe or lets it drift into high-risk structural decay regions.

Plot 3: Psychrometric Chart: This is a thermodynamic snapshot that allows us to see absolute moisture levels in the air, bypassing temperature-dependent variables. An exact thermodynamic target zone (shaped to curve along constant relative humidity tracks between the temperature limits) is displayed.

We decode this complex chart using ALEC: First, identify the Axes. The horizontal axis represents Dry Bulb Temperature ($T$ in $^\circ\text{C}$), while the vertical axis represents the Humidity Ratio or Absolute Moisture ($W$ in $g/\text{kg}$ of dry air). Second, describe the Lines/Points. The curved background lines represent lines of constant $RH$ (curving up to the top-left red $100\%$ saturation boundary line), and the blue markers represent your actual measurements. Third, pick an Example. If you observe data points moving horizontally from left to right, this shows temperature is rising while absolute moisture remains constant—causing $RH$ to drop even though no physical water was actually removed. Finally, form a Conclusion. By tracing where your points fall relative to the curvature, you can identify if there is an active danger of moisture addition leading to condensation or active drying leading to organic brittleness.

Plot 4: Frequency Histograms: This side-by-side view shows the statistical spread of your microclimate variables over the entire log duration. Vertical dashed lines highlight target parameters directly over the occurrences bars.

Using ALEC to interpret histograms: First, look at the Axes. For both plots, the horizontal axis shows the parameter values ($T$ in $^\circ\text{C}$ or $RH$ in $\%$), while the vertical axis shows the absolute frequency or count of hours spent in each bin. Second, observe the Lines/Bars. The height of each vertical column shows how often the environment fell into that narrow range. Third, analyze an Example. A single, tall, sharp column (unimodal peak) represents an exceptionally stable, unwavering climate, while a broad shape or two separate peaks (bimodal) indicates that the room shifts between two distinct states (like heating-cooling seasons or day-night cycles). Finally, establish a Conclusion. Rather than hiding variations behind simple mathematical averages, histograms visually confirm if your collections spend significant periods of time outside safe museum limits.

Plot 5: Period Analysis: This plot aggregates the continuous data into distinct timeframes (months or weeks) to evaluate stability trends. By looking at distributions (Boxplots or isolated IQR bars) or simple medians, you can clearly track seasonal changes, pinpoint specific periods of microclimatic volatility, or analyze how long the environment stays within safety specifications across the year.

Plot 6: Climate State Density Heatmap: This plot simplifies thousands of overlapping points into a clear frequency-concentration grid. The recommended microclimatic compliance region has been outlined in high-contrast blue dashed boundaries.

Let's read this visualization using ALEC: First, note the Axes. The horizontal axis partitions Temperature ($T$) intervals, and the vertical axis partitions Relative Humidity ($RH$) intervals. Second, interpret the Lines/Grid. Instead of points or curves, this is a grid of cells colored from cool green-blue to bright yellow, where brighter colors represent a higher density of concurrent readings. Third, look for an Example. A bright yellow cell at $19\text{ }^\circ\text{C}$ and $48\%$ $RH$ represents the steady-state microclimate where the collection spent the vast majority of its time. Finally, draw a Conclusion. By highlighting density hotspots, rare outlier events (like a door being left open during maintenance) fade into the background, leaving you with an unclouded view of your HVAC system's actual day-to-day performance baseline.

Educational Insight: Robust vs. Parametric Statistics: Traditional metrics (Mean, SD) are highly sensitive to brief spikes—such as sensor calibration errors or opening a door. **Robust statistics** (like Median, Interquartile Range, and the 5th/95th percentiles) give a far more accurate representation of the steady-state conditions that collections actually experience.
